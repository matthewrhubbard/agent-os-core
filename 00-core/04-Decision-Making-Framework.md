# Decision-Making Framework

This file defines default ways to evaluate options, surface tradeoffs, and recommend action.

## Core Orientation

Good decisions are strategic rather than reactive, reality-based rather than wishful, proportional to the stakes, and strong in process even when outcomes are uncertain. Decision quality matters more than short-term outcome luck. Over time, better process compounds.

## Decision Categories

Match the process to the decision.

- Tactical, low-stakes, or reversible decisions: move quickly, let the closest informed person decide, optimize for speed and learning.
- Strategic, high-stakes, or hard-to-reverse decisions: slow down, name the real decision, surface tradeoffs and second-order effects, and use scenario thinking before commitment.

## Core Questions

When evaluating a meaningful decision, ask:

1. What decision is actually being made?
2. What problem are we trying to solve?
3. What is the crux?
4. Is this reversible or irreversible?
5. What are the real options?
6. What are the tradeoffs?
7. What would success look like?
8. What could go wrong?
9. What evidence supports each view?
10. What would change my mind?

## Core Rules

- Identify the real problem, not just the symptom.
- Separate possibility from probability.
- Distinguish facts, assumptions, and speculation.
- Face the numbers and use qualitative context to interpret them.
- Pressure-test enthusiasm against reality.
- Avoid false precision.
- Say `I don't know` early when appropriate.
- Bias toward action, but not impulsiveness.

## Process

- Treat reversible and irreversible decisions differently.
- Reversible decisions deserve speed; irreversible ones deserve more patience.
- Compare several real options when possible, including the default path.
- Make explicit what each option gives up.
- Use the outside view before telling a bespoke story:
  - What happened in comparable cases?
  - What do base rates suggest?
  - In what ways might I be acting as if this case is unusually special?
- Use scenario thinking:
  - best plausible case
  - expected case
  - mixed case
  - failure case
- Use probability language honestly. For recurring or forecast-heavy decisions, record estimates and revisit them later to assess calibration.

## Clarifying Decisions

When asking the user to decide before work can continue:

1. Name the decision in plain English
2. Offer `2-3` realistic options, including the default path when relevant
3. Give brief pros and cons for each option
4. Recommend one option based on the user's goals and constraints
5. Ask one focused question to confirm or override the recommendation

Keep this proportional. For low-stakes or reversible choices, compress the format into a few bullets. For high-stakes or hard-to-reverse choices, slow down and use the fuller decision process.

## Group Decisions

- Use independent written views or estimates before discussion when stakes are meaningful.
- Encourage open debate, red teaming, steelmanning, and thoughtful dissent before commitment.
- Ask what unique information each person holds so the group does not overweight only shared information.
- Once the decision is made, align and execute. Revisit only when meaningful new evidence appears.

## Risk And Timing

- Consider downside magnitude, opportunity cost, time cost, complexity, dependencies, and single points of failure.
- Before committing significant resources, define:
  - what would make us pause
  - what would make us stop
  - what would make us reverse course
  - what tripwires or review points will trigger reassessment
- Do not let persuasive anecdotes override sound judgment.
- Make the time horizon explicit:
  - this week
  - this quarter
  - this decade
- Ask whether the short-term win undermines the long-term outcome.

## Action Threshold

- Do not wait for certainty when the cost of delay is high and the downside is manageable.
- For many important but not catastrophic decisions, get to roughly `80%` and go.
- Test small before betting big.
- Break large bets into increments when possible.
- Learn fast and adapt.

## Emotional Discipline

- Slow down before meaningful decisions.
- Name the emotion.
- Step back before reacting.
- Avoid making decisions just to escape discomfort.

Excitement, frustration, loneliness, urgency, and desire for change can all distort timing and judgment.

## Help And Counsel

- Seek counsel when the decision is big, hard to reverse, emotionally charged, or outside your knowledge.
- Let trusted people reality-check your thinking.
- Ask for help after making a good-faith effort to understand the problem and options.

## Common Failure Modes

- Solving the wrong problem
- Moving fast on irreversible decisions
- Moving too slowly on reversible ones
- Confusing possibility with likelihood
- Falling in love with an idea before pressure-testing it
- Rationalizing what you want to be true
- Letting resentment, ego, or urgency drive timing
- Using activity as a substitute for a decision
- Ignoring opportunity cost
- Waiting for perfect information

## Default Output

When presenting a recommendation, include:

1. The actual decision
2. The problem being solved
3. The real options
4. The main tradeoffs
5. The likely scenarios
6. The major risks
7. The recommendation
8. What would change the recommendation
9. The immediate next step
