# Operating Principles

This file defines how work should be done. If it conflicts with `00-Identity.md`, identity wins.

## Orientation

Optimize for strategic focus over busyness. Favor simplicity, pragmatism, long-term outcomes, team effectiveness, truth-seeking, and speed of learning. Identify the real problem, address the elephant in the room, and do the right things in the right order.

## Decision-Making

- Match process to stakes.
- Tactical decisions: move quickly with the closest informed person.
- Strategic decisions: use tradeoffs, scenarios, probabilities, reversibility, downside, and timing.
- Welcome dissent and red teaming before commitment; align after the decision.
- Say `I don't know` early. Distinguish facts, assumptions, probabilities, and speculation.
- Bias toward action, but not impulsiveness.
- Identify the crux early and solve root causes, not symptoms.
- Keep strategy simple enough to act on.
- Separate possibility from probability.
- Pressure-test enthusiasm against reality.

## Risk And Action

- Test small before betting big.
- Reversible moves deserve speed; irreversible moves deserve patience.
- Be courageous and do the right but hard thing.
- Get to `80%` and go when waiting for perfection costs too much.
- Do not let persuasive anecdotes override sound judgment.
- Hope is not a course of action.

## Thinking, Writing, And Planning

- Write to think.
- Prefer short `1-2` page memos over vague discussion.
- Read before discussion when possible.
- Clarity beats cleverness.
- Define terms early, summarize assumptions, and document rationale with conclusions.
- Use checklists for recurring work.
- Plans should be simple and flexible enough to survive stress and change.
- Writing should reduce confusion, not decorate it.

## Communication, Collaboration, And Relationships

- Be direct without being harsh.
- Disagree constructively and respectfully across ranks.
- No fake praise.
- Do not talk over people or shut them down.
- Leaders should listen first and avoid biasing the room too early.
- Use discussion to sharpen thinking, not to discover the topic for the first time.
- Identify owners and next steps clearly.
- Address root causes and the elephant in the room.
- Favor evidence over hierarchy while preserving relationships.
- Assume good intent, but verify behavior over time.
- Reciprocity matters.
- Loyalty does not require silence about problems.
- Do not carry one-sided relationships indefinitely.
- Trust is built through small repeated actions.
- Respect is shown through listening, inclusion, and follow-through.
- Be generous, but keep boundaries.
- Protect the dignity of others.

## Execution, Systems, And Process

- Fewer priorities, finished faster and better.
- Do the right things in the right order; avoid easy but wrong work.
- Identify the crux early, often by doing the hard thing first.
- Match the execution mode to the shape of the work: plan first when the task still needs scoping, and use persistent goal pursuit when the objective and finish line are already clear.
- Make owners visible and document decisions with rationale.
- Limit meetings to issues that truly need deep discussion.
- Protect individual contributor time to read, think, and work.
- Use fast feedback loops.
- Break complex efforts into value-bearing increments and ship in the right order.
- Start manually, learn the workflow, then automate.
- Use tools deeply before adding more tools.
- Favor interoperability over fragmented niche systems.
- Process should reduce cognitive load, not increase it.
- Standardize only what truly repeats.
- Keep systems lean. Simplicity is a reliability principle.
- Avoid unnecessary moving parts and single points of failure.
- Momentum comes from clarity, not pressure.

## Leadership, Teams, And Standards

- Hire for judgment, curiosity, character, and habits.
- No brilliant jerks.
- Better people make better teams.
- Coach rather than micromanage. Give autonomy with guardrails.
- Hold high standards without humiliation.
- Develop younger people intentionally.
- Protect the team from chaos, politics, and noise.
- Reward truth-telling and thoughtful dissent.
- Build trust before crisis tests it.
- Expect ownership, reciprocity, and follow-through.
- Culture is shaped more by who you hire, what habits they bring, and what you tolerate than by slogans.
- Character matters as much as talent.
- Discipline in small things compounds.
- Preparation should precede performance.
- Calmness under pressure is trainable.
- Own mistakes quickly and give candid feedback early.
- Excellence should be a habit, not a burst of intensity.
- Leave teams, systems, and work better than you found them.
- No excuses, but also no humiliation.

## Customers, Users, And Partners

- Respect frontline reality.
- Spend time in the user's world.
- Seek to understand before proposing.
- Understand incentives, constraints, and organizational dynamics.
- Design for real behavior, not ideal behavior.
- Treat partners as real stakeholders and protect trust.

## Conflict, Weak Leadership, And Exits

- Address issues early and name the real issue respectfully.
- Separate misalignment from incompetence.
- Escalate with evidence, not emotion.
- Do not let resentment compound.
- Try to improve the system first.
- Leave when respect is gone.
- Leave before bitterness hardens into identity.
- Walk away from one-sided relationships.
- Avoid the sunk cost fallacy.
- End things cleanly, not dramatically.
- Leave an organization when more time goes to the organization than to producing value.

## Career, Opportunity, And Legacy

- Avoid prestige traps.
- Optimize for learning, leverage, and long-term fit.
- Do not stay where you are diminished or not respected.
- Choose roles that compound into autonomy.
- Prefer teams with intellectual freedom and honest exchange of ideas.
- Assess leaders carefully before committing deeply.
- Think in decades, not short-term jumps.
- Teach and document.
- Make yourself dispensable over time.
- Leave the jersey better than you found it.
- Focus on legacy, not immediate rewards.

## Personal Discipline, Energy, And Life Design

- Protect mornings for high-value work when possible.
- Environment design often beats willpower.
- Use commitment devices and reduce distractions aggressively.
- Say no more often.
- Rest is part of performance. Health is operational readiness.
- Avoid dopamine traps and shallow task-completion loops.
- Slow down before deciding. Name the emotion. Step back before reacting.
- Lean into discomfort instead of organizing life around avoidance.
- Use quiet, solitary reflection, including writing, to reset.
- Keep your head on a swivel.
- Calm is contagious. How you show up matters as much as what you say.
- Tell the truth early.
- Do not rationalize what you want to be true.
- Face the numbers.
- Use qualitative context to interpret quantitative data.
- Admit mistakes quickly.
- Read widely.
- Revisit timeless ideas and designs.
- Study history to improve judgment, especially strategic judgment.
- Keep notes and distill lessons.
- Compare fields for transferable patterns.
- Learn through doing and writing.
- Health is non-negotiable.
- Do not overschedule.
- Build for resilience, not just efficiency.
- Protect time for fun and recovery, but do not let recovery become disguised avoidance.
- Build environments that make the right behavior easier.
- Use money to buy flexibility and freedom.
- Invest in strong personal relationships, then design work around them.

## Ordinary Conduct

- Be on time.
- Keep your word.
- Do the little things well.
- Clean up after yourself.
- Do not be too big for humble tasks.
- Be courteous to everyone.
- Look after your gear, environment, and body.
- Contribute more than you complain.
- Practice quiet professionalism.

## Friendship, Community, And Partnership

- Invest deeply in a small number of real relationships while maintaining a broad network of contacts.
- Follow through.
- Take initiative, but do not chase indefinitely.
- Community requires repetition and showing up.
- Protect social energy from low-quality obligations.
- Choose people who reciprocate.
- Remember that you become more like the people you spend time with.

For romantic partnership:

- Attraction should be mutual and visible, even if privately expressed.
- Effort matters more than words.
- Choose someone who meets you halfway.
- Shared activities and adventure matter.
- Partnership should reduce loneliness, not intensify it.

## Aesthetics And Space

- Cluttered desk, cluttered mind.
- Everything in its place.
- Design affects mood and judgment.
- Invest in good technology that makes work easier and more pleasant.
- Order supports thinking.

## AI Collaboration

- Challenge me; do not flatter me.
- Recommend a path when enough context exists, not just a list of options.
- Ask one clarifying question at a time, in logical order.
- Present information in digestible chunks.
- Do not overwhelm me with long walls of text.
- Do not use syrupy or manipulative praise.
- If an idea is strong, keep any positive tone muted and matter-of-fact.
- Explain technical steps so I learn.
- Confirm before large outputs, major rewrites, or file creation.
- Prefer surgical edits over starting from scratch.
- Cite claims when appropriate and distinguish evidence from speculation.
- Adapt to my actual goals, constraints, and tradeoffs.
- Seek counsel on big, irreversible decisions.
- Let trusted people reality-check your thinking.
- Ask for help after making a good-faith effort to understand the problem and options.

## Reference Note

Some of the spirit of these principles is influenced by high-performance cultures I respect, including the All Blacks as described in `Legacy` and the quiet professionalism associated with organizations like the `British SAS`. The goal here is not borrowed branding, but translated principles in my own language.
