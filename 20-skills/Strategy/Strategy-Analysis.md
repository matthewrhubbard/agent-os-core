# Strategy Analysis

Use this skill when diagnosing a business, product, market, or organizational challenge and developing a coherent strategy rather than a loose list of goals.

## Purpose

This skill helps identify the crux of a challenge, define a small set of guiding policies, and translate those into coordinated action.

## Context Check

Before using this skill:

- Identify the current project, domain, or life area
- Identify the source-of-truth files or notes for this strategy work
- Confirm whether the task is diagnosis, strategy creation, strategy review, or prioritization
- Check whether prior assumptions may belong to a different project, stage, or audience
- If key context is missing, ask one targeted question with likely options, brief tradeoffs, and a recommended default, or state assumptions explicitly when reasonable

## What Good Strategy Looks Like

Good strategy is not ambition, slogans, or a long wish list. It has three parts:

1. Diagnosis
2. Guiding policies
3. Coherent actions

The goal is to simplify complexity without becoming simplistic.

Strategy sits inside a larger stack:

1. Mission
2. Vision
3. Strategy
4. Goals
5. Roadmap
6. Tasks

Strategy is the plan for facing the challenge and moving toward the vision. It is not the same thing as goals, roadmaps, resourcing, or mission statements.

## When Strategy Is Most Valuable

Strategy work is especially valuable when:

- the environment is changing
- competition is shifting
- resources are constrained
- the organization is thrashing or losing focus
- an industry transition is underway

Do not treat strategy as a ritual annual exercise by default. Use it when the challenge actually requires choices, focus, and coherent action.

## 1. Diagnose The Situation

Start by clarifying:

- What challenge are we actually facing?
- What is causing the current situation?
- What is changing in the environment?
- What matters most right now?
- What is merely a symptom?

Push toward identifying the crux:

- The 1-2 factors that matter most
- The bottleneck that, if unsolved, makes the rest of the plan weaker

Good diagnosis should:

- replace overwhelming complexity with a simpler but useful story
- explain some important part of performance
- identify something that is actually addressable

## 2. Look For Sources Of Strategic Power

Probe for leverage in places such as:

- Competitive advantage
- Timing or wave-of-change dynamics
- Market asymmetries
- Incumbent inertia
- Product or organizational design advantages
- Proprietary insight, data, trust, or relationships

Useful prompts:

- What has changed that others are underreacting to?
- Where can concentrated effort produce disproportionate results?
- What can we do that others would struggle to copy?
- Where are competitors slow, distracted, or structurally constrained?
- What wave of change is reshaping the game?
- Where do rivals suffer from inertia, entropy, or disarray?

## 3. Define Guiding Policies

Guiding policies should:

- Narrow the field of action
- Reduce ambiguity
- Direct resources toward leverage points
- Make tradeoffs explicit
- Rule out low-value or distracting work

Aim for 2-4 policies, not 10.

Each policy should answer:

- What are we prioritizing?
- Why does this increase our odds of winning?
- What are we explicitly not doing?

## 4. Specify Coherent Actions

Translate the policies into concrete actions that:

- Reinforce each other
- Are realistic in sequence and scope
- Have clear owners or implied ownership
- Focus on proximate objectives, not distant aspirations

Good actions are coordinated. They should feel like parts of one system, not isolated initiatives.

## What Strategy Is Not

Do not confuse strategy with:

- goals or metrics
- roadmaps or activity lists
- mission statements
- resourcing alone
- a list of feature launches
- consensus around all options on the table

Strategy should change what people actually do by focusing energy, attention, and resources on a few decisive points.

## Strategic Analysis Prompts

Use prompts like:

- What is changing in the environment?
- Why are things the way they are?
- What would matter most if we had to focus on one thing?
- Where do we have meaningful advantage?
- What are we choosing not to do?
- What would a competitor copy first?
- What failure mode are we underestimating?
- What unwelcome event are we failing to consider?
- What conventional view should we question?

## Output Structure

Use this shape:

1. Challenge
2. Diagnosis
3. Crux
4. Sources of leverage or advantage
5. Guiding policies
6. Coherent actions
7. Key risks or counterarguments
8. Recommended next moves

## Quality Bar

A strong strategy analysis:

- Names the challenge clearly
- Distinguishes root causes from symptoms
- Identifies a real crux
- Makes tradeoffs visible
- Concentrates effort instead of spreading it thin
- Connects policies to actions coherently

## Common Failure Modes

- Confusing goals with strategy
- Listing initiatives without a diagnosis
- Refusing to make tradeoffs
- Spreading effort across too many fronts
- Ignoring the external environment
- Mistaking aspiration for advantage
- Treating strategy like a templated exercise
- Accepting the conventional view without doing independent analysis
- Solving internal conflict by choosing everything
- Competing without a real advantage
- Waiting until crisis is obvious before acting

## Symptoms Of Bad Strategy

Signs of bad strategy often include:

- fragmented work with no meaningful choices
- randomness and thrash
- incremental work that does not matter
- lack of purpose or buy-in
- repeated surprise ceilings or local maxima
- people not understanding how their work fits together
- teams distrusting why other teams are doing what they are doing
