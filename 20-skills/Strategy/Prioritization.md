# Prioritization

Use this skill when there are more worthy tasks, initiatives, or opportunities than available time, energy, or resources.

## Purpose

This skill helps rank work under real constraints and make tradeoffs explicit instead of pretending everything is equally important.

## Context Check

Before using this skill:

- Identify the current project, domain, or life area
- Confirm the actual constraint: time, money, attention, staffing, or sequence
- Identify who the priorities are for
- Check whether any items on the list belong to a different project or horizon
- If key context is missing, ask one targeted question with likely options, brief tradeoffs, and a recommended default, or state assumptions explicitly when reasonable

## When To Use

- Choosing among competing initiatives
- Ranking tasks for a week, quarter, or roadmap
- Deciding what to delay, stop, or ignore
- Evaluating tradeoffs under resource pressure

## Core Principles

- Prioritization is about constrained choice
- A shorter list is often a better list
- Importance and urgency are not the same
- Sequence matters, not just rank order
- Good prioritization includes what not to do

## Workflow

## 1. Define The Frame

Clarify:

- What time horizon matters here
- What resource is constrained
- What objective the prioritization is serving

## 2. List Candidate Work

Capture the real options, then remove duplicates, vague items, and work that does not belong in this decision.

## 3. Evaluate The Items

Assess each item against factors such as:

- Strategic importance
- Urgency
- Downside risk if delayed
- Effort required
- Dependencies
- Learning value
- Optionality created or preserved

## 4. Group Into Tiers

Create practical buckets such as:

- Must do now
- Important, but next
- Nice to have
- Stop, defer, or drop

## 5. Explain The Tradeoffs

State why higher-ranked items beat lower-ranked ones and what is being deprioritized as a result.

## Output Structure

1. Objective and time horizon
2. Constraint
3. Candidate items
4. Prioritization criteria
5. Ranked or tiered list
6. Tradeoffs and rationale
7. Recommended next moves

## Quality Bar

A good prioritization output:

- Matches the actual constraint
- Ranks work in service of a defined objective
- Explains what is being deferred
- Is realistic enough to use immediately

## Common Failure Modes

- Ranking without naming the objective
- Mixing different time horizons in one list
- Treating all good ideas as equal
- Ignoring effort and sequencing
