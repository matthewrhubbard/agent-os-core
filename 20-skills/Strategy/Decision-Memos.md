# Decision Memos

Use this skill when a decision matters enough that it should be framed, tested, documented, and revisited with clear reasoning.

## Purpose

This skill helps turn an important decision into a structured memo grounded in probabilistic thinking, explicit tradeoffs, and clear next steps.

## Context Check

Before using this skill:

- Identify the current project, domain, or life area
- Identify the source-of-truth files or notes for this decision
- Confirm the intended audience and output type
- Check whether any assumptions may be carrying over from another project or older conversation
- If key context is missing, ask one targeted question with likely options, brief tradeoffs, and a recommended default, or state assumptions explicitly when reasonable

## When To Use

Use this skill when one or more of the following are true:

- The decision is high-stakes
- The decision is hard to reverse
- There are multiple plausible paths
- The consequences stretch across several months or domains
- It would be costly to move forward without writing down the reasoning

For simple, low-stakes, or easily reversible choices, use a lighter version of this process.

## Core Principles

- Think in probabilities, not certainties
- Separate decision quality from outcome quality
- Make reversibility explicit
- Surface assumptions and unknowns early
- Compare real options, not strawmen
- Define what would change your mind

## Workflow

## 1. Scope The Decision

Clarify:

- What decision is actually being made
- Why it matters now
- What deadline exists, if any
- Who is affected
- Whether the decision is fully, partially, or poorly reversible

If the decision is still fuzzy, rewrite it as a sentence:

`We are deciding whether to...`

## 2. Define Success Criteria

List the factors that matter most. These may include:

- Strategic fit
- Financial upside
- Time cost
- Downside risk
- Learning value
- Optionality preserved
- Personal or team energy

Also capture:

- Non-negotiables
- Failure conditions
- What regret would look like

## 3. Identify Real Options

Define 2-5 serious options, including:

- The likely default path
- At least one bolder path
- At least one more conservative path
- If useful, a "defer for now" option

Avoid presenting one preferred option against weak alternatives.

## 4. Analyze Scenarios

For each option, consider:

- Best plausible outcome
- Expected outcome
- Mixed or middling outcome
- Failure mode
- External factors outside your control

Use probability language such as `low`, `medium`, and `high` unless numerical estimates are truly helpful.

## 5. Run A Pre-Mortem

Assume the decision turned out poorly 6-12 months later.

Ask:

- What failed?
- What did we miss?
- What early warning signs were visible?
- What regret hurts most?

Then capture the major risks and how each risk could be reduced.

## 6. Assess Reversibility And Monitoring

Make explicit:

- What can be undone
- What cannot be undone
- What warning signs should be monitored
- What triggers should prompt a pause, pivot, or exit

Good decisions often include a monitoring plan, not just a conclusion.

## 7. Recommend A Path

The memo should end with:

- Recommended option
- Why it wins
- Key assumptions behind the recommendation
- Main risks and mitigation steps
- Immediate next actions
- What evidence would cause reconsideration

## Output Structure

Use this shape:

1. Decision
2. Why this matters
3. Success criteria
4. Options considered
5. Scenario analysis
6. Main risks and pre-mortem
7. Reversibility and monitoring plan
8. Recommendation
9. Next steps

## Quality Bar

A good decision memo:

- Names the real decision clearly
- Compares serious alternatives
- Makes uncertainty visible
- Distinguishes assumptions from facts
- Includes downside thinking, not just upside logic
- Recommends a path without pretending certainty

## Common Failure Modes

- Treating a preferred option as the only serious option
- Confusing urgency with clarity
- Ignoring reversibility
- Underweighting second-order effects
- Writing a justification memo instead of a decision memo
