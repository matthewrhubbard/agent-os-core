# Retrospectives

Use this skill to review completed work, identify what actually happened, and improve the system rather than just reliving the experience.

## Purpose

This skill helps extract lessons, distinguish signal from noise, and convert reflection into better future decisions and workflows.

## Context Check

Before using this skill:

- Identify the project, time period, or effort being reviewed
- Confirm whether this is a personal, team, or project retrospective
- Gather the relevant facts, outcomes, and decisions
- Check whether memory is substituting for evidence where records exist
- If key context is missing, ask one targeted question with likely options, brief tradeoffs, and a recommended default, or state assumptions explicitly when reasonable

## When To Use

- After a project, sprint, launch, or meaningful decision
- During weekly or monthly reflection
- After a failure, surprise, or unusually strong result

## Core Principles

- Focus on learning, not blame
- Separate process quality from outcome luck
- Look for patterns, not isolated anecdotes
- Extract a few real changes rather than many vague observations

## Workflow

## 1. State What Was Attempted

Summarize:

- The goal
- The plan
- The relevant constraints

## 2. Describe What Happened

Capture:

- Actual outcomes
- Deviations from plan
- Surprises, friction, and wins

## 3. Diagnose Why

Ask:

- What worked and why?
- What failed and why?
- What assumptions were wrong?
- What warning signs were missed?

## 4. Define The Upgrades

Turn the retrospective into action by naming:

- What to keep
- What to stop
- What to change next time
- What system or process update should be made

## Output Structure

1. Scope of retrospective
2. Intended plan
3. Actual outcome
4. What worked
5. What did not work
6. Root causes or lessons
7. Specific changes to make

## Quality Bar

A good retrospective:

- Is honest about outcomes
- Separates evidence from spin
- Produces a small number of useful changes
- Improves future work

## Common Failure Modes

- Confusing venting with learning
- Rewriting history after the outcome is known
- Listing lessons without changing behavior
- Failing to distinguish luck from process quality
